1. What is CohortIntelligence?

CohortIntelligence is an OHDSI-native cohort exploration workbench. It sits between standard cohort diagnostics (ATLAS, CohortDiagnostics) and formal chart review, providing a data-driven layer for understanding who is in your cohort, what patterns exist, and what questions are worth investigating further.

What it is not CohortIntelligence does not make clinical diagnoses, replace chart review, or establish causal relationships. All outputs are hypothesis-generating, not hypothesis-confirming. Treat every finding as a lead for clinician discussion, not a conclusion.

How data flows

  1. You load a cohort via an ATLAS JSON definition or a pre-built cohort table.
  2. The tool extracts OMOP CDM records for all 7 domains (condition, drug, procedure, measurement, observation, visit, death).
  3. Events are counted per patient × domain × time window, producing a feature matrix for unsupervised machine learning.
  4. UMAP + clustering groups patients by similarity; isolation forest scores each patient's unusualness.
  5. Everything is displayed in six interactive dashboard tabs.

2. Cohort Overview — Quilt Plot

The quilt plot is the centrepiece of the dashboard. It gives a bird's-eye view of the entire cohort in one glance.

How to read it

AxisMeaning
Rows One row = one patient. By default, patients are sorted by cluster assignment so similar patients appear together.
Column groups Each column group is one OMOP domain (CONDITION, DRUG, PROCEDURE, MEASUREMENT, OBSERVATION, VISIT, DEATH).
Columns within a group Each column is a time window relative to the cohort index date (e.g., the first T2DM diagnosis). Negative windows = before index; positive = after. [-12,-6) means 12–6 months before.
Cell colour Intensity = number of events in that domain during that window. Default encoding is log1p(count): white = 0 events, saturated colour = many events.

What patterns mean

PatternClinical interpretation

Quick reading tip Scan the quilt from top to bottom. Rows should cluster into bands of similar colour. Patients that look nothing like their neighbours are worth investigating — they may represent a distinct clinical subtype, data-quality issues, or misclassified patients.

3. Anomaly Explorer

The Anomaly Explorer shows the output of the unsupervised machine learning pipeline applied to the patient feature matrix.

UMAP scatter plot

UMAP (Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection) is a dimensionality reduction algorithm. It compresses hundreds of clinical features (condition × time window, drug × time window, …) into a 2-D map where:

  • Nearby points = similar patients — they share similar clinical histories across domains and time windows.
  • Distant points = dissimilar patients — different clinical histories, different disease trajectories, or different data patterns.
  • Colour = cluster assignment — UMAP clusters visible groups; each colour is one algorithmically identified subgroup.
Important UMAP distances are not linearly interpretable. Two points 5 units apart are not necessarily "twice as different" as two points 2.5 units apart. Focus on the topology (which groups exist, which patients are isolated) rather than exact distances.

Anomaly score

The anomaly score (0–1, higher = more unusual) comes from an isolation forest: a tree-based algorithm that identifies patients whose feature combinations are rare. High anomaly score means the patient's clinical history is difficult to describe with the same rules that describe most other patients.

Score rangeInterpretation
0.0 – 0.4 Typical patient — pattern similar to the majority of the cohort.
0.4 – 0.6 Moderately unusual — may be on the boundary between subgroups.
0.6 – 1.0 Highly unusual — rare combination of clinical features. Warrants clinical review: could be a distinct subtype, miscoded, or have genuine clinical complexity.

How to use it

  1. Look for patients isolated from all clusters (lone points far from any group) — these are the highest-priority anomalies.
  2. Look for small tight clusters away from the main mass — potential rare subtypes.
  3. Click any point to select that patient and view their quilt row and trajectory.

4. Patient Selector

The Patient Selector is a ranked priority queue. It answers the question: "If I can only review N patients, which ones should I look at first?"

Priority score components

ComponentWeightWhat it captures
Anomaly score 50% Isolation forest unusualness (see above).
Cluster noise flag 25% Patients not assigned to any cluster (cluster ID = −1 or 0) receive a bonus — they may represent outliers the algorithm could not classify.
Data sparsity 25% Proportion of (domain × window) cells with zero events. Sparse patients may have incomplete data or represent a qualitatively different care pattern.

Priority tiers

  • High (red) — Top third of the ranked list. Most unusual, sparse, or unclassified. Review first.
  • Medium (amber) — Middle third. Borderline cases or patients on cluster boundaries.
  • Low (green) — Bottom third. Most typical, well-classified, data-rich patients. Useful as controls when interpreting findings.
Use case: clinician meeting preparation Export the top 10–20 high-priority patients as a CSV, pull their trajectories, and use them as discussion cases. This is faster and more targeted than random sampling.

5. Trajectory Viewer

The Trajectory Viewer shows a per-patient swim-lane timeline — all recorded clinical events for a single selected patient plotted against days from their cohort index date.

How to read it

  • X-axis — Days relative to the index date. Negative = before index, positive = after. The dashed vertical line at day 0 is the index date.
  • Y-axis — One row per concept (e.g., "Type 2 diabetes mellitus", "Metformin 500mg", "HbA1c").
  • Each dot — One recorded event. Hover for exact date, value (if available), and concept name.
  • Colour — Domain (red = condition, blue = drug, etc.).

What to look for

ObservationPossible meaning
Dense dots before index, sparse after Patient has a long prior history but limited follow-up — may have transferred care or left the system.
Drug starts immediately after a condition code Treatment initiation — clinically expected (e.g., metformin after T2DM diagnosis).
Measurement dots cluster around drug changes Monitoring behaviour — lab values being checked in response to treatment.
Condition codes appear only before index, never after Possible historical coding (condition coded once, never re-entered) or condition resolved.
Very long span between first and last event Long-term follow-up — useful for longitudinal analyses.
Clinical review tip The trajectory is built from structured EHR data only — it does not include free-text notes, images, or out-of-system events. Treat it as a structured evidence summary, not a complete clinical picture. Always confirm findings with source records before clinical action.

6. Hypothesis Panel

The Hypothesis Panel automatically tests whether any clinical features differ significantly between clusters. Each row is a candidate research question.

Column definitions

ColumnMeaning
cluster_a, cluster_b The two clusters being compared.
domain Which OMOP domain the feature is from (condition, drug, etc.).
concept_name The specific condition, drug, procedure, or measurement being tested.
window_label Which time window showed the difference.
effect_size Magnitude of the difference: rank-biserial correlation (continuous) or Cramér's V (binary). Values closer to 1 indicate a larger difference between clusters.
p_value_adjusted BH-corrected p-value. Smaller = less likely to be due to chance. Treat anything > 0.1 with scepticism; use as a ranking signal rather than a definitive test.
direction Which cluster has more of this feature.
Statistical caution These are exploratory tests on clusters that were defined by the same data. This introduces circularity: you are more likely to find differences between clusters that were constructed to be different. All findings must be validated in an independent dataset or via pre-specified analysis.

How to use it

  1. Click Generate Hypotheses after the cohort has loaded.
  2. Sort by p_value_adjusted to see the strongest signals.
  3. Read each row as: "Cluster A patients have more/fewer [concept] in [time window] than Cluster B patients."
  4. Download the table as CSV and bring it to a clinician meeting for discussion and expert validation.

7. Demographics Tab

The Demographics tab summarises the cohort's population characteristics and data availability.

Value boxes (top row)

  • N patients — Total cohort size after applying all inclusion/exclusion criteria from the JSON definition.
  • Median follow-up — Median time between cohort entry (index date) and cohort exit. Short follow-up limits the ability to observe outcomes.
  • Median age at index — Calculated from year_of_birth and cohort_start_date. Note: OMOP stores birth year, not exact date, so age has ±1 year uncertainty.
  • % with death recorded — Patients with a death event in death domain. This is a lower bound — death capture is often incomplete in EHR data.

Data density heatmap (bottom)

The data density heatmap shows, for each OMOP domain and each calendar month, how many patients in the cohort had at least one event recorded.

  • Horizontal bands — Domains. Brighter = more patients with data in that domain that month.
  • Temporal gaps — White columns indicate months with little or no data. This may reflect:
    • Gaps in data feed to the CDM (EHR data not yet loaded)
    • Periods when the health system was not operational
    • Seasonal variation in care utilisation
  • Domain imbalance — If CONDITION is always bright but MEASUREMENT is mostly white, the CDM may have incomplete lab data.
Interpreting data density for your cohort Before drawing conclusions from any analysis, verify that data density is relatively stable across the observation period. A sudden drop in density near the current date often indicates a data lag, not a real decrease in clinical events.

8. Recommended Workflow

  1. Check Demographics first. Confirm cohort size, age distribution, and data density. If follow-up is very short (<6 months median) or density drops off sharply in recent months, adjust your time window expectations accordingly.
  2. Read the Quilt at a glance. Sort by cluster (default). Do the clusters look visually distinct? Are there groups of unusually sparse patients? Any domains that are uniformly empty across all patients?
  3. Explore the Anomaly Explorer. Identify patients isolated from clusters. Note whether they land in expected or unexpected regions of the UMAP.
  4. Review 3–5 high-priority patients. From the Patient Selector, pick the top high-priority cases. Click each one to load their Trajectory Viewer. Do they look like genuine clinical outliers, or like data-quality issues (very sparse data, impossible chronology)?
  5. Generate hypotheses. Run the Hypothesis Panel. Take the top 5–10 findings to a clinician. The question to ask: "Does this difference between clusters make clinical sense?"
  6. Iterate. Change domain filters, time window ranges, or the sort order. Each view reveals different structure in the data.

9. Limitations & Caveats

LimitationImplication
OMOP CDM is structured data only Free-text notes, imaging, waveforms, and out-of-network events are absent. The "clinical picture" in CohortIntelligence is always partial.
Concepts depend on vocabulary coverage If your institution uses local codes not mapped to standard OMOP concepts, events will not appear. Use check_cohort_json() to verify concept coverage.
Unsupervised ML is sensitive to feature engineering choices Changing time window breaks, minimum concept frequency, or value encoding can change cluster assignments significantly. Results are exploratory, not definitive.
Death capture is often incomplete The death domain only captures deaths recorded in your health system. In-hospital and linkage-based mortality is not captured unless imported to the CDM.
Small cohorts (< 30 patients) UMAP and isolation forest produce unreliable results. Cluster structure may be noise. Hypothesis tests are underpowered.
Circular hypothesis testing Hypotheses are tested on the same data used to define clusters. All findings require external validation.
